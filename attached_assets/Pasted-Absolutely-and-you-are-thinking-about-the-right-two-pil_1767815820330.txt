# 2) Content: what you teach and how you deliver it

Now to the heart of it: your content must match your brand. That means it should feel:

* practical
* friendly
* confidence-building
* conversational
* community-driven

## The Horizonte learning model (simple, repeatable)

I’d structure your content around one weekly rhythm:

### Weekly rhythm (works online + supports in-person)

* **Lesson (10–15 minutes):** one clear topic + useful phrases
* **Daily practice (5–10 minutes/day):** voice notes + micro prompts
* **Conversation Circle (60 minutes/week):** guided speaking in small groups
* **Reflection + mini check (5 minutes):** celebrate progress, set next step

This is the engine that creates real improvement without overwhelming people.

---

## Your program tracks (V1)

To keep “everybody welcome,” I’d keep tracks clear and friendly:

### Track 1: Foundations (Beginners)

Goal: speak in simple sentences without fear.

### Track 2: Confidence (Low-intermediate)

Goal: speak longer, handle real-life situations, improve flow.

### Track 3: English Café (Open Community)

Goal: social practice, friendships, low-pressure speaking.

### Youth version

Same tracks, but:

* more structure
* shorter activities
* stronger safeguarding rules

---

## Your 8-week V1 content plan (community + real life)

This is a strong first cohort sequence that fits “Work & Life” while staying social:

### Week 1: Welcome & Introductions

Names, where you’re from, what you like.
Community goal: everyone speaks on day one.

### Week 2: Daily Life & Routines

Simple present, time phrases, daily habits.

### Week 3: Places in the City (Brasília-friendly)

Directions, locations, asking for help, safe travel language.

### Week 4: People & Relationships

Family, friends, invitations, making plans.

### Week 5: Work & Skills (light, practical)

What you do, what you can do, teamwork language.

### Week 6: Customer Conversations (life + work)

Ordering, booking, asking questions, polite responses.

### Week 7: Problems & Solutions (confidence builder)

Explaining an issue, asking for help, staying calm.

### Week 8: Your Story & Next Horizon

A short “my journey” talk + celebration + next steps.

Each week should include:

* 12–20 key phrases
* 1 sentence pattern (“I usually…”, “I’m going to…”, “Can you help me…?”)
* 1 pronunciation focus (very light)
* 1 community prompt (“Ask two people about…”)

---

## Daily WhatsApp practice (what makes your content come alive)

Your daily prompts should feel like friendly conversation, not school.

Examples:

* “Say hello and share one good thing about your day (20 seconds).”
* “Describe your favorite food and why you like it.”
* “Invite a friend to coffee (use this sentence).”
* “What’s one goal you have this month?”

I would build a library of **56 prompts** for an 8-week cohort so you never scramble.

---

## Conversation Circle format (your weekly script)

A consistent script helps volunteers run great sessions without being expert teachers.

### 60-minute session flow

1. Warm welcome + quick check-in (5 mins)
2. Guided speaking round (15 mins)
3. Pair practice / breakout (15 mins)
4. Real-life roleplay (15 mins)
5. Celebrate wins + next-step challenge (10 mins)

Correction style: gentle and supportive. You correct *patterns*, not every mistake.

---

## English Café (Brasília) content plan

Your Café should have stations that match the week’s theme:

* “Introductions station”
* “City life station”
* “Work talk station”
* “Games station” (the confidence builder)
* “Beginner corner” (protected, slower pace)

People leave feeling: “I spoke more than I expected.”

---

## Placement and progress (simple, not intimidating)

For V1, I’d use:

* self-placement questions (“I can introduce myself confidently / sometimes / not yet”)
* a short voice note sample (optional)
* a simple pre/post speaking rubric:

  * clarity (1–5)
  * length (1–5)
  * confidence (1–5)

Progress should feel encouraging, not exam-like.
